Laura Frick

A data artist that creates installations with the data she collects. People are often afraid of data being collected, however, Frick encourages people to collect as much as possible. When we think of data we think we know what we put out to the public. For instance, we know what we post on social media or what text we sent out etc. But, what we don’t know is a simple object like a Fitbit also collects data and the data obtained is information we probably don’t know about ourselves. From this, Frick expanded her idea into collecting all types of data she could but mainly specified with one and it was sleep data. She used a contraption from Zeo’s that measures your sleep and the headband strap measures your brainwave to identify your sleep state or if you’re awake. She wanted us to see that this of data is a hidden part of you that you don’t see and can learn from it. With the data, she collected she use the color code it with the rule system. Red would mean deep sleep, Yellow is rem sleep and white is trash sleep meaning you got no sleep. She then combined them into something similar looking to a cell membrane. Frick was then able to collect the sleep patterns from different people that used the device and compare them to each other. This then leads her to find out that sleep patterns are very specific to one person as if it were a set of fingerprints since everyone’s is different. In a way, it is a way to define “who you are”. With data tracking, you can discover parts of yourself you don’t know. Overall, her argument in the video is that data isn’t all about fitness or health, it is about who you are.

Stefanie Posavec

As an artist, Stefanie Posavec is a self-proclaimed “data illustrator.” Rather than data visualizer she feels “data illustrator” describes herself better, for contrary to typical data visualizers she does not know how to code html, java, nor javascript. Rather she maps out her data illustrations by hand: physically sketching or drawing in Photoshop. Other than lacking the knowledge to code, she finds her manual labor of data collection and construction attributes a greater emotional and personal quality to her artworks compared to artworks which were constructed by more convenient digital design tools. Also, her methods akin to her obsessive aesthetic. Her process of data collection is impressive, and asinine, which requires her to repeatedly review her subject(s) several times over till she discovers a trend her notice or is fond of. For example, her data-illustration on writing style, Writing Without Words, on one of her favorite books, On The Road, by Jack Kerouae. The data collection took her three weeks where she took meticulous notes and completely color-coated highlighted her copy of the novel. With the data, she illustrated a series of recursive algorithmic designs. One of the series was a tree-diagram which consist its center point to represent the book and continuous chain of numerous branching subdivisions of literary components: starting from book to chapters, paragraphs, sentences and words. Each number of branches represent the amount of components she found in corresponded category and its location marked by the connected previous category. The most interesting one of her series covers the differences between the six different editions of On The Road. The comparison of the six editions documents a color system which illustrates which words up to paragraph survived to the next, later edition (blue = survived, orange = was removed). This makes for interesting design because it’s both informative and similar to how biologist study the passing on of characteristic genes. Concerning On The Road, the illustrations may reveal the characteristics of Jack Kerouae’s writing style. There concept of new representation and informative discoveries generally describes Stefanie Posavec’s artwork. She is a non-coding “data-illustrator” who pursue new scientific mapping design of extensive data sets.
